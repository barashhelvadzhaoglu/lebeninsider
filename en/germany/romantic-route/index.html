About the City / Destination
#

Are you ready to set out on a journey in the heart of Germany where history and fairy tales intertwine? The Romantische Straße, or the Romantic Road in English, is one of the country’s most iconic and enchanting tourist routes, stretching approximately 460 kilometers from Würzburg to Füssen at the foot of the German Alps. This route brings together the most beautiful corners of the Bavaria and Baden-Württemberg states, offering visitors the opportunity to discover medieval towns, majestic castles, lush nature, and a warm culture. The geographical location of the route, situated in the southwestern part of Germany, provides easy access from both the interior of the country and neighboring countries. The cities along the route are generally small to medium-sized, offering a peaceful atmosphere. For example, large cities like Augsburg have a population of a few hundred thousand, while towns like Füssen or Memmingen have populations expressed in the tens of thousands, giving each a unique character.

The story of the route dates back to Germany’s efforts to revitalize tourism after World War II. Established in the 1950s, this itinerary aimed to introduce the country’s cultural heritage and charm to the world by highlighting historical cities, castles, and natural beauties that managed to survive the destruction of the war or were meticulously restored. This strategic move not only repositioned Germany as a tourism destination but also emphasized the country’s cultural diversity and richness. Since then, millions of visitors from all over the world have experienced this unique journey, discovering a different face of Germany. This historic effort has ensured that it remains one of Germany’s most visited tourist routes today.

The Romantic Road connects easily to Germany’s major cities. Füssen, the southern end of the route, is approximately 130 km from Munich, taking about 2 hours by train and 1.5 hours by car. Würzburg, the northern end, is about 120 km from Frankfurt, 1.5 hours by train and 1 hour by car. This makes it very practical to start from any point on the route or visit specific cities. Its location in the southwest of Germany also places it close to neighboring countries like Austria and Switzerland, making it an attractive spot for those planning a broader European tour.

The character of the route, as the name suggests, is built on romance and nostalgia. Every town, every castle, and every landscape you encounter along the way seems as if it has jumped out of a fairy tale book. From the wine vineyards along the Main River in the north and the baroque splendor of historic Würzburg to the breathtaking views of the Bavarian Alps and the fairy-tale atmosphere of Neuschwanstein Castle in the south, the route offers a constantly changing but always fascinating tableau. While wandering through narrow, cobblestone streets, getting lost among colorful half-timbered houses, or looking down at the valley from the ramparts of a castle, you feel as if time has stopped. German hospitality, local flavors, and an organized, peaceful atmosphere turn this route into not just a trip, but a soul-resting experience. The trees blooming in spring and leaves turning golden yellow in autumn transform this route into a paradise for photographers, while sitting in outdoor cafes and enjoying the sun in summer is a separate pleasure. The weather generally shows a typical Central European climate; mild and occasionally rainy in summers, cold and snowy in winters. Especially the people of the Bavaria region are known for their warm-blooded and tradition-bound nature.

So, why should you go to the Romantic Road? If you want to see that Germany consists of more than just big cities like Berlin or Munich, to breathe the magic of medieval Europe, to listen to the stories of magnificent castles, or simply to discover some of Europe’s most beautiful rural landscapes, this place is for you. This route offers a perfect synthesis of history, culture, and nature, promising unforgettable memories for travelers of all ages. Whether you travel by bicycle, by car, or like us, with the Deutschlandticket and a stroller, the Romantic Road promises you unforgettable memories and inspiring experiences. This is not just a road, but a living history book that connects the past with the present, promising a new discovery at every corner.

Places to Visit
#

The Romantic Road connects 29 cities and towns, each with its own story and beauty. Countless castles, palaces, historic city walls, Gothic churches, Baroque palaces, and natural beauties await visitors along this route. Our experience focused on cities that can be reached by day-trip train services from Munich and can be easily explored with children. Here are the cities and key points we personally visited and experienced, which best reflect the spirit of the route:

🏛️ Augsburg — A City of Finance and Art from Roman Times
#

Augsburg (Google Maps), which is only 35 minutes away from Munich by train, is one of the largest cities on the Romantic Road and the third-largest city in Bavaria. Founded by the Romans in 15 BC, this city holds the distinction of being the second oldest city in Europe. It became a rich trade center in the Middle Ages and one of Europe’s largest financial centers during the Renaissance. Hosting powerful merchant dynasties like the Fugger family, it became a sort of European banking center. The city’s historic center is quite comfortable for families with its wide streets and green areas.

  • Fuggerei
    #

    The world’s oldest social housing project still in operation, the Fuggerei (Google Maps), was founded in 1521 by Jakob Fugger for poor Augsburg citizens. It provides the opportunity to live for a symbolic rent of only 0.88 Euro per year, forming an example of social responsibility and philanthropy for over five centuries. With its orderly rows of houses, small courtyards, and unique atmosphere, the Fuggerei offers a journey through a time tunnel. While wandering through the quiet courtyards of the Fuggerei, we were very impressed by this atmosphere where history and social consciousness intertwine. It also offers a safe and calm area for children to run around; since it is an enclosed area, you can let them loose comfortably. The entrance fee is symbolic (approx. 2 Euro) and it can be visited between 08:00-20:00 in summer and 09:00-18:00 in winter. This unique place reveals a social assistance model extending from ages ago to the present day.

  • Rathaus (Town Hall) and Goldener Saal
    #

    The symbol of Augsburg, this magnificent town hall is a Renaissance masterpiece built between 1615-1620. The Rathaus (Google Maps) is considered one of Europe’s most important secular Renaissance buildings. The “Golden Hall” inside (Goldener Saal (Google Maps)) has a fascinating beauty with its ceiling decorated with gold leaf, magnificent frescoes, and natural light filtering through its large windows. When we went, there was a small event in the hall, and the children’s eyes were fixed on the carvings on the ceiling; they felt like they were in a fairyland. This hall, which impresses visitors not only with its architecture but also with the artworks it contains, symbolizes the city’s wealth and fondness for art. The entrance fee is about 3-4 Euro, and it is generally open between 10:00-18:00.

  • Perlachturm
    #

    Located right next to the Rathaus, this bell tower offers the best panoramic view of the city with a height exceeding 70 meters. You reach the Perlachturm (Google Maps) by climbing 258 steps, and it is a magnificent experience to see Augsburg’s historical texture, the Fuggerei, and the distant Alps from a bird’s-eye view, especially on spring or autumn days. The city’s red-roofed houses and green parks offer a distinct beauty when viewed from above. Since we were with a stroller that day, we didn’t climb the tower, but I definitely recommend it for young travelers or those fond of views. The tower is generally open between 10:00-17:00 and may close depending on weather conditions. The entrance fee is around 2.50 Euro.

  • Augsburg Dom (Cathedral)
    #

    Augsburg Dom (Google Maps), this Gothic cathedral home to some of Europe’s oldest stained glass windows, is one of the city’s most important buildings religiously and architecturally. With origins dating back to the 11th century, it bears the traces of a thousand-year history. Inside the cathedral, you will find a peaceful atmosphere and can examine historical artworks. We took a short tour with the children, taking care not to break the silence inside, and observed the impressiveness of the massive stained glass. The cathedral’s garden is also ideal for a short break. Entrance is free, and it is generally open between 09:00-18:00.

🏰 Füssen — The City in the Shadow of Neuschwanstein
#

Füssen (Google Maps), which you can reach with an approximately 2-hour train journey from Munich, is the last stop on the Romantic Road in the south. Established at the foot of the Alps, by the side of the turquoise Lech River, this charming town has many beauties waiting to be discovered on its own, not just for its proximity to Neuschwanstein Castle. While everyone rushes to the castles, it would be a big mistake to skip Füssen’s historical texture, narrow streets, and peaceful atmosphere by the river. Especially in autumn, the town view combined with the color feast of the mountains and trees fascinates people.

  • Hohes Schloss (High Castle)
    #

    Rising above Füssen, this late Gothic castle is one of the city’s most striking structures. The famous courtyard of the Hohes Schloss (Google Maps) with its deceptive paintings (trompe l’oeil) and the terraces offering magnificent views of the city and the Lech River are worth seeing. There is also a museum inside the castle showcasing the city’s artistic heritage. We spent some time in the castle’s courtyard and enjoyed the city view; it was a good start for the children’s first encounter with historical structures. Especially the illusion paintings on the exterior facade triggered the children’s questions like “Is there really a window here?”, leading to fun moments. Generally open from 11:00-17:00, entrance fee approx. 5 Euro.

  • St. Mang’s Abbey and Füssen City Museum
    #

    Located by the Lech River, this former Benedictine monastery is a beautiful example of Baroque architecture. The city museum inside St. Mang’s Abbey (Google Maps) showcases Füssen’s history and lutherie tradition (especially violin making). We took a break in the monastery courtyard to let the children burn off some energy; it has a peaceful and wide area. Especially the grand interiors of the monastery church and the intricacies of Baroque architecture impress visitors. In the museum, you can find detailed information about Füssen’s fame in musical instrument making.

  • Lech River and Lechfall
    #

    The turquoise Lech River passing through Füssen contributes greatly to the city’s natural beauty. Lechfall (Google Maps) is an impressive spot where the river turns into a dramatic waterfall and forms a small canyon. Watching this natural beauty from the bridge and feeling the power of the water was quite enjoyable. It can be a refreshing stop, especially in the summer months. The children were as if mesmerized while watching the violent flow of the water, which became an unforgettable moment for us. The walking tracks by the river are also ideal for spending a calm day.

👑 Neuschwanstein Castle — Disney’s Inspiration
#

Accessible from Füssen with just a 10-minute bus or minibus journey, Neuschwanstein Castle (Google Maps) is one of the world’s most famous and photographed castles, built in the 19th century by Bavarian King Ludwig II. It is said to have been the inspiration for Walt Disney’s Cinderella and Sleeping Beauty castles. This is not just a castle, but also a romantic vision and the embodiment of imagination. This fairy-tale structure in the middle of the Alps takes on a different beauty every season.

  • Neuschwanstein Castle Visit
    #

    Tours of Neuschwanstein Castle (Google Maps) are conducted only as guided tours, and it is a must to buy tickets online in advance or early in the morning from the ticket offices in Füssen (Ticketcenter in Hohenschwangau); otherwise, you face the risk of long queues or tickets being sold out. Due to our climb to the castle with a stroller (the walking path is quite steep) and the narrow stairs inside the castle, it was not possible for us to join the tour. However, even the views from the road leading to the castle and from Marienbrücke were worth this experience alone. Even if we couldn’t see the inside of the castle, admiring it from the outside was enough, especially the children’s reaction was priceless. Entrance fee approx. 15 Euro, opening hours vary by season (generally 09:00-18:00). For our detailed guide: Neuschwanstein Castle Travel Guide.

  • Marienbrücke (Mary’s Bridge)
    #

    This is a suspension bridge overlooking the castle, where its most iconic photos are taken. From Marienbrücke (Google Maps), it is possible to see both the magnificent view of Neuschwanstein and the depth of the Pöllat Gorge. The bridge can be reached on foot or by bus. Especially with the afternoon light, the castle view is wonderful. Passing with a stroller might be a bit challenging due to the narrowness of the bridge and the crowds. Provided you are careful with the children, it offers a great photo spot. The bridge can be quite crowded, especially in the summer months, so going in the early hours can provide a more comfortable experience.

  • Hohenschwangau Castle
    #

    Located slightly below Neuschwanstein, this is the palace where Ludwig II spent his childhood. Although Hohenschwangau Castle (Google Maps) is not as famous as Neuschwanstein, it is worth seeing for its interior decoration, lake view, and more personal atmosphere. Being located in the village of Hohenschwangau makes it easy to visit on the same day as Neuschwanstein. This place has a warmer and more familial atmosphere and is filled with orientalist and romantic paintings from the period of Ludwig II’s mother, Queen Marie.

🏙️ Memmingen — Authentic Medieval City Most Don’t Know
#

Memmingen (Google Maps), about 1 hour away from Munich by train, is one of the hidden gems on the Romantic Road. Surrounded by completely preserved medieval city walls, this city offers an authentic Bavarian experience away from crowded tourist groups. Here you can find historical texture, silence, and peace all together. The city’s narrow streets, half-timbered houses, and quiet squares make you feel as if you’ve jumped out of a fairy tale book.

  • Marktplatz (Market Square)
    #

    The heart of the city, this square is surrounded by historic buildings, cafes, and shops. Marktplatz (Google Maps) can be pleasant to visit, especially for the markets set up here in the summer months. It was quite comfortable to wander around the flat and wide square with a stroller. The town hall and merchants’ houses located in the square reveal the city’s rich past. It also offers a safe area for children to run around comfortably.

  • Siebendächerhaus (House of Seven Roofs)
    #

    One of Memmingen’s most interesting buildings, the Siebendächerhaus (Google Maps), draws attention with its seven-story roof. It used to be a house where craftsmen and especially tanners lived. This unique architecture reflects the original character of the city and is a great spot for taking photos. Small canals and historic bridges nearby also make it more attractive.

  • Ulmer Tor (Ulm Gate)
    #

    A part of Memmingen’s well-preserved city walls, the Ulmer Tor (Google Maps) is a beautiful example of the city’s medieval defensive architecture. Walking along the city walls is a great way to experience the city’s history from a different perspective. While walking here, you can feel like a knight or a medieval merchant. The green areas around the gate are suitable places for children to play and for taking a break.

🌊 Lindau — Island City on Bodensee
#

Lindau (Google Maps), which you can reach from Munich with an approximately 1.5-hour train journey, is a unique city established on a small island on Bodensee (Lake Constance), resembling a Mediterranean town with its palms and Alpine views. It is ideal for visiting especially in the summer months, and although it deviates slightly from the Romantic Road, it is definitely worth seeing. The turquoise waters of the lake, the Alps in the background, and the colorful buildings in the foreground offer postcard-like views.

  • Lindau Harbor
    #

    With the Bavarian lion statue and lighthouse that have become symbols of the city, Lindau Harbor (Google Maps) offers one of the most beautiful views of Bodensee. Sitting on a bench here and enjoying the lake and the Alps opposite is priceless. The children’s joy accompanied us while eating ice cream around the harbor, and watching the boats come and go was a great amusement for them too. Climbing the lighthouse offers a 360-degree view of the city and the lake; it is open from 10:00 am to 6:00 pm in the summer months, and there is a small entrance fee.

  • Maximilianstrasse
    #

    The main shopping and living street of the island city, Maximilianstrasse (Google Maps), is quite lively with its colorful buildings, shops, and cafes. You can take a pleasant walk here, browse local handicrafts, or take a break in a cafe. The historical buildings and ornate facades along the street also offer many opportunities for photographers. It is quite suitable for wandering with a stroller and has wide sidewalks.

  • Old Town Hall (Altes Rathaus)
    #

    Decorated with colorful frescoes, this building is one of the most striking historical structures on the island. The detailed paintings on the facade of the Old Town Hall (Google Maps) tell Lindau’s rich history and cultural heritage. The square in front of the building is also ideal for taking a small break and watching the surroundings. This building is a unique example where Gothic and Renaissance architecture are blended.

  • Bodensee Tour
    #

    With boat tours departing from Lindau, you can visit other beautiful cities of Bodensee (e.g., Bregenz, Konstanz) or simply take a pleasant tour on the lake. Lake tours are a great option for both resting and enjoying the view from different angles. Especially in the summer months, ferries organize frequent trips, and you can also cross with a bicycle. The parks by the lake also offer great opportunities for spending time with children.

Other Romantic Road Cities We Haven’t Visited Yet But Are on the List
#

The Romantic Road contains much more than the cities we listed above. Here are a few cities we haven’t been able to visit yet but are on our “must-go” list:

  • Rothenburg ob der Tauber
    #

    The most iconic city on the route, Rothenburg ob der Tauber (Google Maps), is like an open-air museum. It has a fairy-tale atmosphere with its completely preserved medieval city walls, narrow, cobblestone streets, and half-timbered houses. It is also known for its Christmas-themed shops and famous Christmas Markets throughout the year. It will definitely be added to the list and might be the star of our next Deutschlandticket adventure. The city’s night watchman tour offers visitors a fun experience full of medieval stories. This is an indispensable stop for anyone who wants to take a time journey in medieval Europe.

  • Dinkelsbühl
    #

    Known as the quieter and less touristy version of Rothenburg, Dinkelsbühl (Google Maps) has similar beauty with its city walls, towers, and historic houses. It is considered one of Germany’s best-preserved medieval towns and is a hidden gem waiting to be discovered. It is said to be stroller-friendly, which is a big plus for us. The half-timbered houses in pastel tones and quiet squares of the city also offer great shots for photographers.

  • Würzburg
    #

    The northern starting point of the route, Würzburg (Google Maps), is famous for its magnificent Residenz palace on the UNESCO World Heritage List, the historic bridge over the Main River (Alte Mainbrücke), and its vineyards. The Residenz, one of the most beautiful examples of Baroque architecture, is a must-see along with its palace gardens. It is ideal for those looking for a different starting or ending point and is a paradise for those who love wine tasting. Walking by the Main River and examining the statues on the bridge are also pleasant activities.

  • Nördlingen
    #

    Nördlingen (Google Maps) is a unique city located inside a massive crater formed by the impact of a giant meteorite. It is known as one of the only towns in the world established inside a crater. The city walls are completely walkable, and from here, it offers a magnificent experience to look at the town and the impact crater (Ries Crater) from a bird’s-eye view. Understanding the history of this geological formation makes the city even more interesting. The tower of St. Georg’s Church called “Daniel” is a perfect point to watch the view of the city and the crater.

When we go to these cities, our detailed articles containing our experiences and practical notes will take their place on Share-Hub-EU. Discovering each city’s unique charm, architectural details, and local lifestyle makes the Romantic Road truly unique.

Where to Eat? Featured Restaurants
#

In every city along the Romantic Road, it is possible to find many restaurants, cafes, and beer halls where you can try the exquisite flavors of Bavarian cuisine. While traveling with children, bakeries (Bäckerei) offering quick and delicious snacks and street flavors also offer great options. Especially traditional Gasthofs stand out by offering both affordable and authentic dining experiences. Here are recommendations selected from our experiences and the general route:

Featured in Augsburg:

  • Augustiner am Dom (Google Maps): Located right next to the Augsburg Cathedral, this traditional Bavarian restaurant is known for its authentic atmosphere and delicious regional dishes. Especially the roast pork (Schweinebraten), Munich schnitzel, and local beers are great. Since it has a wide space, it is also comfortable to go with children. There are also vegetarian options on its menu.
    • Type: Traditional Bavarian, German Cuisine
    • Price Range: €€ (Medium, main courses 15-25 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Authentic Bavarian experience, central location, wide menu, child-friendly atmosphere, and friendly service.
  • König von Flandern (Google Maps): A stylish restaurant combining German and European cuisine with a modern touch. Ideal especially for dinners and appeals to those looking for more refined flavors. Its menu, prepared with seasonal products, offers fresh and creative dishes. Its interior decoration is also quite contemporary and pleasant.
    • Type: German, European Cuisine, Modern
    • Price Range: €€€ (Upper Medium, main courses 20-35 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Stylish atmosphere, innovative flavors, central location, and quality wine list.

Featured in Füssen:

  • Gasthof Krone (Google Maps): Located in a historic building in the center of Füssen, this traditional inn offers both accommodation and dining services. Its quality regional dishes, especially schnitzels, Bavarian steaks, and desserts, are highly liked. The outdoor seating area is ideal for experiencing Füssen’s lively atmosphere in the summer months. Children’s menus and baby chairs are also available.
    • Type: Traditional Bavarian, German Cuisine
    • Price Range: €€ (Medium, main courses 14-24 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Central location, delicious local dishes, historical atmosphere, children’s menus, and great service.
  • Ristorante Pizzeria Il Pescatore (Google Maps): A great Italian option for those looking for a different taste in Füssen. Quite popular with its pizzas cooked in a wood fire and fresh seafood. Pizza with children is always a safe and loved option! It draws attention with its friendly environment and fast service. The place also blends Italian cuisine with local tastes by including lake fish in its menu.
    • Type: Italian Cuisine, Pizza, Seafood
    • Price Range: €€ (Medium, pizzas 10-16 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Great pizzas, family-friendly, friendly staff, and fast service.

Featured in Lindau:

  • Gasthof Alte Post (Google Maps): Located in the historic island center of Lindau, this Gasthof offers flavors from traditional Bavarian and Swabian cuisine. It is especially known for its lake fish specialties. The restaurant’s authentic interior decoration and warm atmosphere promise a pleasant dining experience. The centrality of its location is a great advantage for travelers.
    • Type: Traditional Bavarian, Swabian Cuisine, Fish
    • Price Range: €€€ (Upper Medium, main courses 18-30 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Local fish specialties, historical atmosphere, central location, and quality local wines.
  • Valentino (Google Maps): This Italian restaurant located near Lindau Harbor offers delicious Italian food accompanied by a great lake view. It is popular with its fresh pastas, seafood, and wide wine menu. Ideal for a romantic dinner or a stylish family meal. Especially eating while accompanied by the lake view at sunset offers an unforgettable experience.
    • Type: Italian Cuisine, Seafood
    • Price Range: €€€ (Upper Medium, main courses 20-35 EUR)
    • Why Recommended: Lake view, quality Italian flavors, stylish ambiance, and wide menu option.

Having morning breakfasts at an authentic Bäckerei or Konditorei with fresh bagels, croissants, and German coffees is a great way for an energetic start to the day.

Accommodation
#

Accommodation options along the Romantic Road are quite diverse, ranging from boutique hotels to traditional inns (Gasthof), from family pensions to modern hotel chains. Where you stay depends on how you plan the route. Since we did day trips based in Munich, we didn’t need accommodation, but I can provide some suggestions for those who want to discover the whole route or stay longer in specific cities. Early reservation, especially in the summer months and during the Christmas markets period, offers both better prices and more options.

General Accommodation Strategies:

  • Regional Centers: If you are traveling the route from north to south or vice versa, it may make sense to stay in large cities like Würzburg, Augsburg, and Füssen. These cities serve as good bases for organizing day trips to smaller towns around them and usually offer more transportation options. You can also find more restaurant and entertainment options in these cities.
  • Medieval Towns: Spending a night in authentic medieval towns like Rothenburg ob der Tauber or Dinkelsbühl is a great way to experience the atmosphere fully. When the tourist crowds disperse in the evenings, these towns take on a truly magical air, and walking through their calm streets is a unique experience. However, accommodation prices in these towns can generally be a bit higher.
  • Family-Friendly Options: If you are traveling with children, Gasthofs (inns) or aparthotels offering apartments (Ferienwohnung) or wide family rooms might be more suitable. Apartments offering the possibility of preparing your own food can be both practical and budget-friendly for families with children and offer more living space.

Which Region is Better to Stay?

If you plan to visit the entire route, it will make sense to determine one or two main bases. This way, you get rid of the trouble of changing hotels every day and can discover a specific region more deeply. Keeping the frequency of hotel changes to a minimum when traveling with children means a more comfortable journey for everyone.

  • For the North: Würzburg or Rothenburg ob der Tauber can be good starting points. From these regions, you can discover the Main River valley and vineyards. Würzburg is a larger city and offers more accommodation and transportation options.
  • For the Center: Cities like Augsburg or Dinkelsbühl are located in the center. Augsburg, as a large city, offers more modern facilities and fast transportation, while Dinkelsbühl is attractive for those looking for a quieter and more authentic medieval town experience.
  • For the South: Füssen or Schwangau (location closest to the castles) is ideal. This region is perfect for breathtaking views of the Alps and lake activities. Especially for visiting Neuschwanstein Castle, this is the most suitable location.

Pensions and Ferienwohnungs (holiday apartments) offer great options for longer stays and budget-conscious families. These usually offer more space and kitchen facilities, providing flexibility to families.

#

The Romantic Road is one of Germany’s most accessible tourist itineraries and can be easily explored with various transportation methods. Our choice was train travel with the Deutschlandticket due to its flexibility and economic nature with children. Thanks to Germany’s excellent infrastructure, transportation is quite comfortable.

Route Transportation Methods:

  1. Car (Most Flexible Option):
    • Advantages: It is the most flexible method to discover the whole route, the smallest villages, and overlooked views. You can stop at your own pace and take photos, making spontaneous decisions. For those traveling with children, it also provides great ease in carrying belongings. Especially Germany’s highways (Autobahn) are of high quality and offer a comfortable drive for long distances. Roads between villages are also generally in very good condition. Rental cars can usually be easily found at airports or major city centers.
    • Disadvantages: Especially in medieval towns (Rothenburg, Dinkelsbühl), finding a parking place and parking fees can be a problem, and in some areas, vehicle traffic may not even be allowed. Narrow streets and crowds can make driving difficult. Fuel costs and car rental fees should also be considered. Also, while driving in Germany, it may be useful to examine topics like the Green Insurance Guide and Can you Drive in Germany with a Bulgarian License?. The LPG Guide in Europe may also contain important information, especially for LPG car owners.
  2. Train (Economic and Eco-Friendly with Deutschlandticket):
    • Advantages: Thanks to Germany’s wide railway network, it is possible to reach many cities on the route by train. Especially with the Deutschlandticket, you can use all regional trains (RE, RB, S-Bahn) unlimitedly for 49 Euro per month, significantly reducing costs. For families with children like us, the stress factor is lower; children have the opportunity to move in the train and watch outside from the window. German train stations usually have elevators and ramps, which makes traveling with a stroller easy and provides barrier-free access. For more information on this, you can check our Barrier-Free Life in Germany article.
    • Disadvantages: Not all cities on the route are directly connected by train. Bus transfers may be required to reach some small towns. High-speed trains (ICE) are not covered by Deutschlandticket, which can prolong travel time over long distances, and transfer times need to be set well.
    • Is Deutschlandticket valid? Yes, we used the Deutschlandticket for all the cities we went to (Augsburg, Memmingen, Füssen, Lindau). For local buses departing from Füssen to reach Neuschwanstein Castle, it may be necessary to pay an extra fee, but the train journey is completely covered. Cities like Rothenburg ob der Tauber can also be reached by train and with transfers with the Deutschlandticket. You can find more details in our Trip Around Munich with Deutschlandticket article.
  3. Romantic Road Bus (Romantische Straße Bus):
    • Advantages: It is a special bus service covering the entire route. It stops at all important cities and towns from Würzburg to Füssen, so you can experience the whole route without the trouble of driving. It can offer informative stops and guided tours. These buses are generally comfortable and offer room for large luggage too.
    • Disadvantages: Usually, it only has specific trips in the summer months (April-October). Ticket prices can be higher than the train and are not covered by Deutschlandticket. Offers less flexibility because you have to stick to the bus schedule and there is no chance to reach smaller, out-of-the-way points. Reserving tickets in advance is recommended.
  4. Bicycle:
    • Advantages: The closest way to discover nature and small villages. Many bicycle paths are found along the route, and Germany being generally a bicycle-friendly country provides an advantage. It is an eco-friendly and healthy way of traveling. Bicycle rental options are also available in many cities.
    • Disadvantages: Requires physical effort and may not be suitable for long distances with children. Depends on weather conditions, and the amount of belongings you will carry with you is limited. There may be slopes on the route in some regions, which can make bicycle riding difficult.

Urban Transportation:

Most cities and towns on the Romantic Road, especially the old city centers (Altstadt), are pedestrian-friendly and can be easily discovered on foot.

  • Walking: Walking is the best way to discover the narrow streets, historical buildings, and hidden corners of small towns. This also allows you to feel the local atmosphere in the best way. The chance of encountering unexpected beauties while walking also increases.
  • Stroller: Main streets and parks in big cities (Augsburg, Lindau) are quite suitable for strollers. However, the cobblestone streets of medieval cities like Rothenburg or Füssen can be a bit challenging for large vehicles like twin strollers. The road leading to Neuschwanstein Castle is very steep and long for a standard stroller, and the buses reaching the castle might be a better option. Still, a stroller offers great comfort to avoid having to carry children in arms.
  • Local Buses/Trams: Public transport (tram, bus) is quite developed in large cities like Augsburg and allows you to move easily within the city. These vehicles can generally be used with the Deutschlandticket or daily/weekly tickets. Public transport vehicles are generally designed suitable for disabled and stroller access.

Whatever transportation method you choose, the Romantic Road promises you a journey full of unforgettable views and memories. The important thing is to make a planning suitable for your own travel style and especially their needs if you are traveling with children.

Our Experience
#

Discovering the Romantic Road has been for us not just a trip, but also a proof of how differently and richly we can experience Germany with the Deutschlandticket, two children (ages 1 and 5), and a twin stroller. Deviating from traditional tourist routes and reaching these fairytale cities with day-trip train services from Munich was the right decision for us. Not having the trouble of changing hotels and returning to our own bed every evening provides priceless comfort when traveling with children and we don’t disrupt their routines too much.

What Did We Like Very Much?

  • Freedom and Economy of the Deutschlandticket: Being able to travel unlimitedly on all regional trains for 49 Euro a month became the key to a budget-friendly family trip. We were fascinated by being able to decide spontaneously which city to go to every morning, the train journeys themselves turning into entertainment, and German railways generally being punctual. Elevators at stations and wide stroller sections on trains made it incredibly easy for us to move with our twin stroller. While the children watched outside from the window, accompanying the rhythmic sound of the train’s wheels, we also enjoyed the changing landscapes of Bavaria, lush fields, and distant mountains. This was a journey full of discovery and fun rather than tiredness. Thanks to this ticket, we had the opportunity to discover cities we had never thought of before and had the chance to chat with people from different corners of Europe on trains. You can find more tips on this in our Trip Around Munich with Deutschlandticket | Children’s Bavaria Routes article.
  • Hidden Beauties and Discoveries: Cities like Memmingen (Google Maps) and Lindau (Google Maps) turned out to be far beyond our expectations. While wandering among the ramparts in Memmingen’s quiet medieval streets, we deeply felt that authentic Bavarian spirit. In these quiet corners, away from the crowds, we felt the city whispering its own story. Lindau’s island atmosphere on the shores of Bodensee, with its palms and Alpine views, gave us a short Mediterranean escape. Eating ice cream at the harbor there and watching the ducks swimming in the lake was one of the children’s favorite moments. The lighthouse and lion statue at the harbor opened the door to a fairyland. Discovering such places coincided with our “sharing culture” philosophy; Share-Hub-EU’s aim is to find little-known but full-of-experience places.
  • The Joy of Discovering with Children: Seeing these historic cities through the eyes of children was a completely different experience. Their running around in the quiet courtyards of the Fuggerei in Augsburg, their admiration for the turquoise waters of the Lech River in Füssen, their surprise at the majesty of Neuschwanstein Castle… Every moment turned an ordinary trip into an unforgettable family adventure for us. Especially our elder son asking “Does a princess live here now?” every time he saw a castle added a fairytale dimension to our journey. Although there were some challenges of wandering with a stroller, the energy and joy the children added to this journey were worth everything. Their desire to discover and their reactions to every new thing gave our journey a completely different meaning and provided us with different perspectives.
  • Diversity and Accessibility of Germany: The Romantic Road showed that Germany consists of not just large cities, but also lush rural areas, majestic mountains, and fairytale towns. Every city had a unique character and story; one day we breathed Roman history, and the next day we enjoyed the Alps by a lake. This diversity made our trip much richer and exciting. Also, as Barrier-Free Life principles are generally adopted in Germany (Barrier-Free Life in Germany: Ease of Transportation from Stroller to Wheelchair), transportation was quite easy even for those traveling with strollers or the elderly.

What Was Below Expectations / What Were the Challenges?

  • Accessibility of Neuschwanstein Castle: Although the castle itself is fascinating, the difficulty of climbing to Marienbrücke with a twin stroller and not being able to enter the castle with a stroller created a bit of disappointment. As the castle road is already a challenging slope and the walking distance is long, it becomes more of a viewing-from-afar experience for families with small children. The sudden change in weather can also make this slope even more difficult. Still, this didn’t subtract anything from the magic of Neuschwanstein; even looking from afar was enough to fascinate one. You can find all the details and alternatives in our Neuschwanstein Castle Travel Guide article.
  • Cobblestones of Medieval Towns: Although train and station access is great throughout the route, the cobblestone streets in the centers of some medieval cities were quite challenging for twin strollers. We predict this situation will be even more pronounced in famous towns like Rothenburg ob der Tauber that we haven’t visited yet. Pushing a stroller on this type of ground is both tiring and can disrupt the children’s comfort, even waking a sleeping baby. For this reason, it may be more sensible to choose a smaller and lighter stroller or use a baby carrier in these types of towns.
  • Weather Variability: Since Bavaria is located at the foot of the Alps, weather can be quite variable, especially in spring and autumn months. While one day is sunny, the next day can be rainy or cool. For this reason, we always had to dress in layers and keep raincoats with us. Children also need to be prepared against suddenly changing weather conditions. Checking weather forecasts frequently and being flexible against sudden changes should be an important part of travel planning.

This adventure taught us not only the beauties of Germany but also the pleasure of discovering new places as a family and overcoming difficulties together. We know that we can go to many more places with the Deutschlandticket and will continue to discover the hidden beauties of Germany. This route promises a journey full of unforgettable memories for both history buffs and nature lovers. Maybe in our next adventure, we will discover cities like Augsburg, Füssen, or Memmingen in more detail!

Frequently Asked Questions
#

1. When is the best time to visit the Romantic Road? Generally, the spring (April-May) and autumn (September-October) months are ideal. The weather is milder, nature becomes colorful, and tourist crowds are less than in the summer months. Especially in autumn, vineyards and forests take on golden yellow colors. Summer (June-August) can be hot and crowded, while winter (November-March) offers Christmas markets and snowy landscapes, but working hours of some tourist places may be restricted and the weather can be quite cold.

2. How many days should be allocated for the Romantic Road? To tour the entire route comprehensively, spending at least one day in each city, it is ideal to allocate at least 5-7 days. If you are going to visit specific cities with day trips like us, you can plan a full day for each city. It would be a good idea to allocate at least one full day for popular places like Rothenburg ob der Tauber or Neuschwanstein, especially considering castle tours and crowds.

3. Is English spoken on the Romantic Road? Yes, in tourist areas, hotels, restaurants, and big cities, you usually come across staff who speak English. However, in smaller towns or when communicating with locals, knowing basic German words (like hello, thank you, please) and using a translation application will make your job quite easy. English translations are usually found in menus too.

4. Is the Romantic Road safe? Germany is generally a very safe country, and cities on the Romantic Road also fit this generalization. There is no large crime rate, and you can wander comfortably in city centers even at night. Still, it is always a good idea to be careful with your personal belongings and take precautions against pickpocketing in crowded places. It is recommended not to take your eyes off children in crowded areas.

5. Is it suitable to tour the Romantic Road with children? Absolutely yes! The Romantic Road offers a great experience for children with its fairytale castles, historical cities, and plenty of open space. Playgrounds and parks are found in abundance. However, if you are traveling with a stroller, you may experience some challenges like cobblestones and climbing to Neuschwanstein Castle. Generally, traveling by train with the Deutschlandticket and day trips is quite comfortable and flexible for families with children.

6. What are the visa and currency information? Germany is a member of the Schengen Area, so a Schengen visa is required to enter the area. For detailed information about visa processes, you can look at our Schengen Visa Application Process (2026) article. The currency is Euro (EUR). Although credit cards are valid in most places, it is recommended to keep some cash with you for small shops, markets, or some small cafes.

7. Is it easy to find public toilets in the cities on the route? Toilets are usually found in large train stations and shopping centers, but some may be paid (approx. 0.50-1 Euro). Most restaurants or cafes offer toilet facilities for their customers. In some cities, there are public toilets (WCs) you can use for a small fee. As this situation is quite important when traveling with children, giving a toilet break while sitting in a cafe and drinking something is a practical solution.

8. Which city on the route is the least crowded? Generally, cities like Memmingen and Dinkelsbühl do not draw as dense tourist crowds as Neuschwanstein or Rothenburg ob der Tauber. These towns are great options for those looking for a more authentic and quiet medieval town experience. You may also have more chances to interact with locals here and can experience a more local atmosphere.

9. Is it possible to rent a bicycle and is the route bicycle-friendly? Yes, you can find bicycle rental shops in many large cities on the route. The Romantic Road has specially marked, mostly flat, and well-maintained bicycle paths for cyclists. This makes it quite suitable for discovery by bicycle. Especially in spring and summer months, riding a bicycle is a great way to enjoy nature.

10. How is Wi-Fi access in the cities on the route? It is possible to find free Wi-Fi access in most hotels and restaurants. Public free Wi-Fi points may also be available in centers of big cities or at train stations. However, internet access may be more limited in small towns, so it will be useful to keep a mobile internet package with you or use an eSIM.
